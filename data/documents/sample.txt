================================================================================
                    THE CHRONICLES OF RAANPUR AND THE GHATS
================================================================================

Raanpur is a quiet village tucked away in the Western Ghats. The village life
revolves around three distinct locations on Station Road: Blue Pottery Arts,
The Monsoon Café, and the ancient Devpur Library. Over the years, the village
has expanded into two neighboring districts: the hillside market of Malhar Heights
and the river valley settlement of Kudremukh Creek.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
PART 1: THE BLUE POTTERY MASTERS AND THE HIDDEN VAULT
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Blue Pottery Arts is owned by an artist named Devendra Joshi, who learned the
craft from his grandfather, Harichand Joshi. In 2015, Devendra created his
masterpiece: a cobalt-blue vase etched with peacocks, named "The Monsoon Blossom."
For seven years, he kept it proudly on display in the shop's front window, where
it attracted tourists from Mumbai and Pune.

However, a crisis occurred in November 2022. A large troop of wild langurs entered
the veranda, breaking several terracotta pots and causing chaos. To keep the precious
masterpiece safe, Devendra moved "The Monsoon Blossom" to a velvet-lined wooden box
hidden directly under the billing counter. The vase is strictly taken out for
public viewing only once a year, on the specific day of Kartik Purnima.

In January 2024, Devendra’s niece, Ananya Joshi, joined the shop as an apprentice.
She introduced a new line of emerald-green clay mugs called "Sahyadri Cups." These
mugs sell for ₹350 each. However, if a buyer purchases a set of six, Ananya applies
a 15% discount, rounding the total price down to ₹1,785, but this offer is only
valid on weekends.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
PART 2: THE SECRET RECIPE DISPUTE AND THE COMPETITION
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
The Monsoon Café sits right opposite the pottery shop and is run by a fiery chef
named Meera Nair. Her most popular item is "Kapi-Chai," a unique blend of South
Indian filter coffee and spiced Masala tea. From the café's opening in 2018 until
early 2024, a standard cup of Kapi-Chai cost exactly ₹40.

In June 2024, due to a sudden, severe rise in global cardamom and fuel prices,
Meera hiked the price to ₹55. This price increase sparked a massive argument
with the local auto-rickshaw union, who used to frequent the café during their
afternoon breaks. To prevent a boycott, Meera offered a specific compromise:
union drivers can get a ₹10 discount (making the cup ₹45), but only if they
physically show their official yellow permit badge to the cashier before ordering.

Meanwhile, in the neighboring district of Malhar Heights, a rival café named
"The Mist view Lounge" opened in October 2024. It is owned by Meera’s former
assistant, Rohan Shetty. Rohan sells a similar drink called "Chai-Kapi" for ₹60.
To compete, Rohan offers free biscuits, but only to college students who present
a valid university ID card.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
PART 3: THE FORBIDDEN MANUSCRIPT OF DEVPUR
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
At the far end of Station Road is the Devpur Library, managed by a dedicated
archivist named Professor Kabir Shastri since 2008. The library houses a rare
palm-leaf manuscript from the year 1742 titled "The Stars of Sahyadri." This
fragile manuscript contains ancient astronomical maps and weather forecasting techniques
used by 18th-century farmers.

Because of its historical value, Professor Shastri keeps it locked inside a heavy,
double-bolted teakwood cupboard located in the damp basement of the library. The
key to this cupboard is hidden inside a vintage brass tiffin box, which sits on
top of an old, non-functional Godrej typewriter in his main office.

The library rules regarding this document are incredibly strict. Regular visitors
cannot simply walk in and view it. To see the manuscript, an individual must first
obtain an official written permission letter stamped by the Raanpur Gram Panchayat.
Furthermore, photography is completely banned; visitors are only allowed to take
notes using a graphite pencil provided by the library staff.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
PART 4: THE CHUGU RIVER FLOOD OF 2025
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Every year, the Chugu River, which runs parallel to the village, swells during the
heavy monsoon season. On July 14, 2025, a continuous cloudburst caused the river
to cross the danger mark at exactly 4:00 PM. This resulted in minor flooding across
the entirety of Station Road, threatening the village's cultural assets.

Emergency protocols were immediately put into action:
1. Professor Shastri rushed to the basement and successfully moved all of the
   library's historical books, including the palm-leaf manuscript, up to the dry
   first floor.
2. Devendra Joshi locked his pottery shop early and gathered his family to help
   the neighborhood youth build sandbag barriers along the riverbank.
3. Meera Nair kept her café kitchen running and distributed free, hot cups of
   Kapi-Chai to all the local rescue volunteers and emergency workers.

By midnight, the water levels began to recede, and no permanent damage was reported.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
PART 5: THE LOST ARTIFACT OF KUDREMUKHK CREEK
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Down in the valley at Kudremukh Creek, a different mystery exists. A retired fisherman
named Raghav Rao discovered a rusted iron anchor buried in the river silt in August
2023. Local folklore suggests the anchor belonged to a legendary trading boat called
"The Sahyadri Queen," which disappeared during a storm in 1899.

Raghav stores the anchor in his backyard tool shed behind a pile of old nylon fishing
nets. He plans to donate it to the Devpur Library museum once they build a new maritime
wing. Until then, he charges curious tourists a small fee of ₹20 to view it and take
photographs. However, residents of Raanpur village are always allowed to view it
completely free of charge.
